The Great Reform — An Overview

The Temperance Movement stands as one of the most sweeping reform campaigns of the nineteenth century, drawing together Protestant clergy, women's rights advocates, and working-class reformers under the singular banner of abolishing strong drink from American life.

Beginning in earnest in the 1820s, preachers such as Lyman Beecher condemned alcohol as the root cause of poverty, domestic violence, crime, and moral degradation. His Six Sermons on the Nature, Occasions, Signs, Evils, and Remedy of Intemperance (1827) became the movement's founding text.

By mid-century, temperance had transformed from a voluntary pledge into a political crusade. The passage of Maine's prohibition law in 1851 inspired a dozen states to follow suit. Women proved indispensable — denied the ballot, they organized "pray-ins" at saloons, formed the mighty WCTU, and pioneered tactics that would later define suffrage and progressive-era politics.

The movement's most dramatic moment came with the 18th Amendment (1919), yet its ultimate legacy is contested: Prohibition generated organized crime, speakeasies, and widespread contempt for law — leading to its repeal in 1933.

Key Terms

  • Temperance — moderation in or abstinence from alcohol
  • Prohibition — legal ban on manufacturing/selling alcohol
  • Saloon — public drinking establishment; symbol of moral decay to reformers
  • Pledge — written vow to abstain from alcohol
  • WCTU — Women's Christian Temperance Union
  • Maine Law — 1851 statewide prohibition statute
  • 18th Amendment — constitutional prohibition of alcohol (1919)
  • Speakeasy — illegal drinking establishment during Prohibition

Why It Mattered

Temperance was the first mass social movement to win a constitutional amendment. It linked moral reform to political action, empowered women as organizers decades before suffrage, and reshaped debates about government's right to regulate private behavior.

      Political Cartoon Analysis
      ✦ ✦ ✦

      Two period cartoons examined for symbols, bias, and persuasive intent

      Cartoon I — "The Drunkard's Home" (c. 1846)
      🔍 Symbols Identified

      Liquor bottle = moral corruption; weeping woman = innocent victim; broken objects = domestic destruction; dark doorway = moral abyss

      📣 Characters & Roles

      Staggering husband (villain / cautionary figure); suffering wife and children (sympathetic victims used to provoke emotion)

      ⚖️ Bias / Exaggeration

      Deliberately exaggerates poverty and anguish to maximize emotional impact; omits context of economic hardship or social conditions

      🎨 Visual Techniques

      Dark vs. light contrast (sinister doorway vs. innocent domestic space); scale of man made imposing and threatening to family

      Overall Propaganda Meaning: This cartoon functions as a morality tale aimed at middle-class Protestant audiences. By centering the suffering of women and children, it casts temperance not merely as personal virtue but as protection of the family — the sacred unit of American society. The drunkard is not portrayed as sick but as a moral agent choosing destruction, making the audience's sympathy entirely with the family and their demand for prohibition.
      Cartoon II — "King Alcohol and His Slaves" (c. 1850)
      🔍 Symbols Identified

      Giant bottle crowned as king = tyrannical power of alcohol; chains = addiction and social enslavement; crusader figure = righteous liberation

      📣 Characters & Roles

      "King Alcohol" as monstrous oppressor; chained workers as victims of addiction; temperance figure as a revolutionary liberator

      ⚖️ Bias / Exaggeration

      Frames drinking as literal enslavement — a provocative parallel to the abolitionist movement; portrays all alcohol drinkers as helpless victims

      🎨 Visual Techniques

      Scale exaggeration (giant bottle dwarfs humans); monster facial features on inanimate object; contrast between enslaved and the heroic crusader

      Overall Propaganda Meaning: By depicting "King Alcohol" as a literal tyrant with chains, this cartoon deliberately borrows the moral vocabulary of the anti-slavery movement, suggesting that drinking is a form of bondage from which Americans must be freed. This appealed powerfully to northern Protestant audiences already sensitized to abolition rhetoric. The temperance crusader rendered as a liberating hero positions legislation not as government intrusion but as an act of national liberation.

      Original Propaganda Posters
      ✦ ✦ ✦

      One promoting the movement — one opposing it

      ✦ Poster I — Pro-Temperance ✦
      Effectiveness Analysis This poster creates support for temperance by centering the family home as the value under threat. The warm golden house and family silhouettes appeal to Protestant ideals of domestic virtue, while the crossed-out bottle signals that rejection of alcohol = protection of everything sacred. The red banner catchphrase — riffing on a familiar proverb — elevates women from passive victims to active defenders of national morality, which was both rhetorically empowering and politically strategic for the WCTU, which linked temperance reform directly to women's suffrage.
      ✦ Poster II — Anti-Prohibition Opposition ✦
      Effectiveness Analysis The opposition poster counters temperance by invoking liberty — the foundational American value — as the frame for resistance. A Lady Liberty figure holding a beer mug and breaking chains labeled "Prohibition Law" repositions anti-temperance not as defending vice, but as defending constitutional freedom. This was the core rhetorical strategy of "Personal Liberty" leagues, particularly among German and Irish immigrant communities who resisted nativist Protestant morality being imposed through law. The warm amber palette and triumphant composition create a sense of celebration rather than sin, making drinking appear natural and resistance to prohibition patriotic.
